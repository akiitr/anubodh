**Summary:** New research indicates that excessive protein intake beyond moderate levels (approx. 0.55-0.72 g/lb body weight) yields diminishing returns for muscle growth, emphasizing that consistent training and sufficient total calories are more critical drivers, while high protein can be costly and reduce adherence.

## Key Takeaways

*   **Protein is not the sole driver of muscle growth:** While essential, excessive protein intake beyond moderate levels does not proportionally increase muscle gains. Research suggests a flat trend for growth beyond a certain point.
*   **Moderate intake is often sufficient:** For most lifters, 0.55-0.63 g of protein per pound of body weight is enough to maximize muscle growth, with an upper range of 0.73 g/lb for those dieting or seeking maximum assurance.
*   **Training and Calories are paramount:** Hard, progressive training and adequate total calorie intake are the primary drivers of muscle hypertrophy and fat loss, with protein playing a supporting role.
*   **Excess protein has drawbacks:** Consuming more protein than needed can be expensive, less satiating for weight gain, potentially interfere with hormone balance or glycogen stores, and lead to GI discomfort.
*   **Protein's specific benefits:**
    *   **Supports growth:** Moving from very low intake to moderate levels significantly aids muscle repair and growth.
    *   **Dieting aid:** Higher protein intake is crucial during calorie restriction (especially below 15% body fat) to manage hunger and preserve muscle mass.
    *   **Adherence:** For some, protein-rich foods and supplements are a convenient and enjoyable way to meet targets.
*   **Protein is often found in other foods:** Typical diets often include protein from sources like bread, beans, and peanut butter, meaning daily targets might be met without extreme measures.
